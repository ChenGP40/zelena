Geothermal Energy
What is Geothermal Energy?
Geothermal energy is thermal energy in the Earth's crust: a combination of energy from the planetary formation and from radioactive decay. To harness this power, heat pumps take heat from one location to another through the use of electricity. These systems work because the temperature roughly 30 feet below the ground remains relatively constant throughout the year, which means that temperatures underground are cooler than above in the summer and are warmer underground in the winter. Hence, a heat pump can be used to efficiently exchange the temperature between these two locations no matter the time of year.
Prerequisites:

Factors such as geology, hydrology, and land availability should be considered before installing a geothermal heater. Soil type, rock type, how deep water is beneath the ground, infrastructure of the area, and etcetera will heavily determine the cost and system available in your location. Depending on these variables, different types of systems and pumps could be considered until the best match is found for you.

Costs:

Costs for geothermal systems highly vary depending on the type of pump installed and the location. Different types of areas require different systems. Some of these include closed loop system, open loop systems, closed loop vertical systems, closed loop horizontal systems, pond systems, and various hybrid systems. An average sized home can expect to pay anywhere from $12,000 to $30,000 total. However larger homes may require $30,000 to $45,000.

Financial Incentives:

Unfortunately, the initial cost of installation is several times that of a normal system. However, it can be paid back quickly because there are less maintenance costs, they last longer, and produce more energy than a normal system. Some can save you up to 50% on your water-heating bill by preheating tank water, which cuts energy consumption by 20%-50% and reduces maintenance costs.
